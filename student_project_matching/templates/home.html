Match me a project

Once upon a time there was a University concerned about how to match students to projects were they would be the most ambitious and excited to work in the upcoming months together with the dedicated tutors that would guide them achieve their goals. However, they encountered a significant challenge on this mission.

What was the challenge, you ask? Well, they faced a matching problem. This means that both students and tutors have preferences that may not align perfectly, and there are also limited capacities to consider.

To make this process as efficient and transparent as possible, we created this website to assist students and tutors in finding suitable matches for specific projects based on their preferences and requirements.

If you're already familiar with the process, feel free to click directly on "Go to matches" to begin your matching journey. Otherwise, keep scrolling to learn more about how this matching process works behind the scenes, or visit our GitHub page for additional details.

Stable Matching (easy explained)

Given a set of n proposers (students) and recipients (projects), each with their own preferences, our goal following the Gale-Shapley algorithm was to to find an assignment such that:

Each student ends up with exactly one project.

In this case a project may allocate more than one student, but not vice versa. This is known as a stable matching. Its applications range from economics and networks to medical school assignments. The Gale-Shapley Algorithm proposes a greedy solution to this. We process students arbitrarily and go down each proposer's list of preferences for recipients. In each round, if a recipient is free, this (proposer, recipient) pair becomes temporarily "engaged". If the recipient is already engaged, it will leave (and thus free) its current partner only if it prefers this proposer more. This process continues until all n proposers and recipients are engaged with one other person. It is proven to produce a unique and optimal solution. It runs in quadratic time.

For more information visit gale-shapley.com.
